When people build a personal homepage, the first instinct is often to put everything on it: projects, skills, articles, contact methods, social links — ideally all visible at once.

But the pages that stay with people are usually not the ones with the most information. They are the ones that know exactly what they want to say.

![Homepage information structure sketch](/assets/illustration-grid.svg "Homepage information structure sketch | I prefer a page structure that establishes hierarchy first and breathing room second, instead of just filling every area with information.")

## Start with one question: what do you want people to remember?

If a visitor only stays for thirty seconds, what is the clearest impression they should leave with?

- You are someone who understands products
- You are good at making complexity feel clear
- You publish and think in public
- You are seriously shaping your own way of working

Once that answer is clear, the page structure usually becomes much easier to decide.

## The biggest risk is having a little bit of everything

A little bit of everything can look complete, but in practice it often means nothing stands out. This is especially true for the homepage.

A homepage usually only needs to do three things well:

1. Tell people who you are
2. Show what you are working on lately
3. Offer obvious next steps

The detailed experience, archive, and project explanation can all live on inner pages.

## Good language is part of the experience

A lot of templates do not feel weak because the visuals are plain. They feel weak because the language has no human texture.

If a homepage says something like “welcome to my digital universe” or “I create stunning experiences,” it often sounds inflated. A more natural version is usually much simpler:

> I work across product, design, and frontend experience, and I also write down what I am learning along the way.

It is not dramatic, but it feels more believable.

## Closing

A good personal homepage does not need to be complicated, but it does need to be chosen. The information has been arranged, the tone has been weighed, and the visuals know when to step back.

Once it can represent you steadily, it stops being just a template.
